Why We Protect & How It Works

Foundation Module — Required for All Volunteers, Staff, and Leaders
SG-T-001 Version 3.0 March 2026 Bethany Chapel
⏱ Estimated reading time: 10 minutes
This is the starting point for every volunteer, staff member, and leader in ministry at Bethany Chapel. It covers why protection matters, what your role is, what the six core protections are, and what you need to know about staying current.
Section 1

Why We Protect

Scenario

It’s 9:15 on a Sunday morning. Parents are dropping off their children, greeting you with a smile, and heading into the service. They don’t think twice about it. They trust you. They trust this church. They trust that their child will be safe, cared for, and returned to them unharmed.

That trust is one of the most sacred things a church holds. It is also one of the most fragile.

What does it take to be worthy of that trust?

The Biblical Mandate

Safeguarding is not a modern invention bolted onto church life. It is woven into the character of God and the commands of Scripture.

“Give justice to the weak and the fatherless; maintain the right of the afflicted and the destitute. Rescue the weak and the needy; deliver them from the hand of the wicked.” — Psalm 82:3–4 (ESV)

God does not ask us to notice the vulnerable. He commands us to defend them. The verbs are active and urgent: give justice, maintain, rescue, deliver. There is no passive option.

“Whoever receives one such child in my name receives me, but whoever causes one of these little ones who believe in me to sin, it would be better for him to have a great millstone fastened around his neck and to be drowned in the depth of the sea.” — Matthew 18:5–6 (ESV)

Receiving a child is receiving Christ himself. Harming a child brings the severest judgment Jesus ever pronounced. The children and families who walk through our doors do not belong to us. They belong to God. We are stewards—entrusted with something precious for a limited time.

Why Structure Matters

The Reality

Most abuse is committed by someone the child knows and trusts—not a stranger. The absence of a system does not mean the absence of risk. It means risk is invisible.

“It won’t happen here” is the most common sentence spoken by organizations where it already has.

This is not about fear or suspicion. A shepherd builds a fence not because he hates the sheep, but because he knows the wolves are real. Good structure does two things: it makes it very hard for anyone with bad intentions to gain access, and it makes sure that the good people serving—people like you—are never put in an ambiguous or vulnerable position. The system protects the kids. It also protects you.

Who We Protect

The Safeguard Framework Protects Everyone

Children and youth — from harm, exploitation, and neglect.

Vulnerable adults — from abuse and loss of dignity.

Volunteers and staff — from false accusations and ambiguity.

Families — from broken trust.

The church — from legal liability and loss of witness.

Clear procedures, visibility requirements, and documentation standards mean you are never in a situation where your word is the only evidence of what happened. When you follow this system, you serve with confidence—knowing exactly what is expected and exactly how you are covered.

Section 2

Your Role as a Volunteer

Your job is straightforward: care for the kids, follow the system, and speak up if something doesn’t feel right. You are not an investigator. You are not a security guard. You are a trusted adult who creates a safe, fun, welcoming environment—and who knows what to do if that environment is ever disrupted.

  1. Follow the procedures. The modules that follow will walk you through each one. They are simple and specific. When you follow them, the system works.
  2. Stay visible. Keep doors open, stay where others can see you, and never be alone with a child. This protects the kids and protects you.
  3. Speak up if something concerns you. If something feels wrong—a child says something that worries you, or a situation doesn’t seem right—tell your ministry leader. That’s it. You don’t need to investigate or be certain. You just need to say something.

You are never in trouble for raising a concern.

You are always in trouble for staying silent about one.

This is both a moral principle and a legal reality under Alberta law.

Reporting a concern almost never happens—and that’s a good thing. The whole point of this system is to create an environment where problems are extremely unlikely in the first place. But if something ever does come up, you need to know that your only job is to speak up. The later module on reporting (SG-T-003) will cover this in full detail.

Section 3

Section 4

Staying Current

You went through the screening process to get here. But screening is not a one-time event—it is an ongoing commitment. There are three things you need to know about staying current:

Annual Renewal

Every ministry year, you must complete a refresher training and sign a policy acknowledgement confirming you have reviewed and understand the current standards. If your renewal is not complete, you cannot serve—regardless of how long you have been volunteering. A four-year veteran and a brand-new volunteer are held to the same standard.

Police checks renew every three years. The Safeguard Team tracks this for you and will let you know when yours is coming due. Bethany Chapel covers the cost.

If you lapse, you pause. This is not punitive—it is simply how the system works. Complete your renewal and you are back on the schedule. The Safeguard Team and your ministry leader will help you get current quickly.

Section 5

The System Behind You

You don’t need to understand the architecture of the Safeguard Framework. That’s leadership’s job. What you need to know is that there is a system behind you, and it works.

Policies define the rules—what must always happen and what must never happen.

Procedures and checklists are your quick-reference tools. They are posted in classrooms, near bathrooms, and in every ministry area so you always know what to do.

Forms capture the record that things happened the way they should. Sign-in sheets, attendance logs, incident reports—they exist so nothing depends on memory.

Training is the layer you are in right now. It teaches you how everything works so you can follow it with confidence.

Here is what this looks like on a normal Sunday:

Scenario — A Normal Sunday Morning

You arrive and check the Room Capacity Card posted by the door to confirm how many kids your room can hold. Parents sign their children in on the attendance sheet. You and your co-leader run the program together, keeping the room visible with the door open or the window uncovered. After the service, parents sign their children out. You tidy up, note anything worth mentioning to your ministry leader, and head home.

That’s it. Every action traces back to a procedure you were trained on. The system works because you followed it.

When you follow the system, you are never guessing. You know what is expected. You know where to find answers. And if anything unusual ever comes up, you know who to talk to. This is not bureaucracy. This is your shield.

Section 6

This Is an Act of Worship

When you protect a child, you honor the God who made them. When you follow a procedure, you love the family who trusted you. When you speak up about a concern, you defend someone who cannot defend themselves.

Safeguarding is an act of worship.

This is not bureaucracy. This is faithfulness.

You are now ready to begin the rest of your training. Each module that follows will teach you one piece of the system in detail. But you already know the foundation: why we protect, who we protect, what the six core protections are, and that there is a proven system behind everything you do.
