Incidents, Compliance & Oversight

Leader Track — Module 3 of 3
SG-T-103 Version 1.0 March 2026 Bethany Chapel
⏱ Estimated reading time: 14 minutes
This module covers two sides of the same coin: what to do when things go wrong (incidents and abuse allegations), and how to make sure they don’t (walkthroughs, variance logs, and compliance audits). Your volunteers handle the first response. You own the follow-through and the prevention.
Section 1

Incidents and Accidents

Scenario

A toddler falls off a low chair and bumps her head during the service. Your volunteer applies ice, comforts the child, and tells you at pickup. The child seems fine. The parent thanks you and leaves.

It’s handled, right? Not yet. Without documentation, this never happened—until someone claims it did, and you have no record.

A child falls. A toddler bites another child. A preschooler has an allergic reaction. These are not crises, but they all require documentation and communication. Your job when a volunteer reports an incident:

  1. Confirm the child is safe and has received appropriate first aid (SG-SOP-006).
  2. Ensure the volunteer completes SG-FRM-006 (Incident/Accident Report) the same day. Review it for completeness—facts only, no opinions, no diagnoses.
  3. Notify the parents at pickup. Be factual, be calm, be specific about what happened and what you did.
  4. For serious injuries (anything requiring medical attention beyond basic first aid), notify the Safeguard Team.

Follow-up matters. Use the follow-up section of SG-FRM-006 to track what happens next: parent communication, leadership review, any changes to procedures. An incident report without follow-up is a liability, not a record.

Section 2

Abuse Allegations and Disclosures

Scenario

A volunteer approaches you after the children’s program looking shaken. “A little girl told me something. I think it’s abuse. I already called Child & Family Services like the training said. I need help.”

Your volunteer did their job. Now you need to do yours.

Your volunteers are trained to follow the reporting steps: ensure safety, report to authorities, notify you, and document. By the time an allegation reaches you, the initial response may already be underway. Your role is not to start the process—it is to support it, secure it, and follow through.

Your Mandatory Reporting Obligation

Under Alberta law, if you personally have reasonable grounds to suspect abuse, you must report to Child & Family Services or Police—regardless of whether someone else has already reported. If a volunteer reports to you and you now share those reasonable grounds, you have an independent legal duty to report. Do not assume someone else’s report covers yours.

The First 60 Minutes

  1. Support the reporter. The volunteer who received the disclosure is likely shaken. Affirm them: “You did the right thing. Thank you for acting.” Ask if they have already contacted authorities. If yes, confirm it was done. If not, help them make the call now. Do not take over the report—it belongs to the person who witnessed or received the disclosure.
  2. Ensure your own report. If the information gives you reasonable grounds, make your own report to Child & Family Services or Police. You do not need proof. You do not need certainty. Reasonable grounds are enough.
  3. Secure the environment. If the accused person is present, remove them from all contact with children immediately. This is not a judgment of guilt. It is a protective measure. Do it calmly, privately, and without explanation to other volunteers or parents. Say only: “We need to speak with you separately. Please come with me.”
  4. Notify the Safeguard Team and Executive Pastor. This notification happens after the external report, not before. Provide facts: who reported, what was said, what actions have been taken so far.
  5. Preserve confidentiality. Limit information strictly to the Safeguard Team, the Elders (as required), and civil authorities. Do not discuss the situation with other volunteers, parents, or staff unless specifically authorized by police.
  6. Ensure documentation. The volunteer completes SG-FRM-007 (Suspected Abuse Report) the same day. Review it for completeness. The form must include the child’s exact words—verbatim, not summarized. Use the follow-up section to track every subsequent action.

What You Must Not Do

Do not investigate. You are not police. You are not a social worker. Gathering evidence, questioning the child further, or confronting the accused can compromise the investigation and harm the child.

Do not notify the parents if a parent is the suspected abuser. Let authorities handle that contact.

Do not delay. Do not wait for the service to end, for the Safeguard Team to meet, or for “more information.” Reports go to authorities immediately.

Do not discuss the situation publicly. No announcements, no group emails, no corridor conversations. Confidentiality protects the child, the reporter, and the integrity of the investigation.

Do not speak to media. If contacted by a journalist, say nothing and direct them to the Executive Pastor or Elders. One sentence: “I’m not authorized to comment. Please contact our church office.”

After the First 60 Minutes

The immediate crisis will pass. The follow-through will not. Track every action taken: when authorities were contacted, when the accused was removed, when parents were notified (if appropriate), what the Safeguard Team decided, and what changes were made to prevent recurrence.

Care for your people. The reporter needs pastoral support—receiving a disclosure is traumatic. The accused, if a member of the community, will need to be managed with both firmness and compassion—removal from ministry does not require cruelty. And you will need support yourself. Talk to the Executive Pastor or a trusted elder. Do not carry this alone.

The Principle

You are never in trouble for reporting in good faith. You are always in trouble for not reporting. This applies to leaders exactly as it applies to volunteers.

Section 3

Walkthroughs

Scenario

You walk past the elementary room and notice the door is closed. Through the window, you see one adult on her phone while the other manages the group. The room is at capacity.

Two issues: the door should be open, and one adult is not supervising. Both are correctable in 30 seconds with a calm, direct word. That is what a walkthrough is for.

A walkthrough is a brief, routine check of your ministry area during a program. It takes five minutes. It is the single most effective tool you have for catching small problems before they become big ones.

Walk through at least once per service. Check: are both adults present and engaged? Is the door open or the window unobstructed? Is the sign-in sheet being used? Does the headcount match the capacity card? Are children supervised—not just present, but actually being watched?

Frame walkthroughs as normal leadership presence. If volunteers feel surveilled, you will get compliance theater instead of genuine safety. Pop in, say hello, scan the room, and move on. If you see something, address it warmly and directly. Save formal documentation for patterns, not first-time observations.

Section 4

Reading Variance Logs

Your volunteers were trained to Pause → Merge → Log when coverage drops. Those logs come to you for review within 24 hours. Here is how to read them:

A single variance is information. A volunteer’s co-leader was late. They paused check-in, called for help, and logged it. That is the system working. Acknowledge it. Thank them for documenting it. Move on.

A pattern is a problem. The same volunteer logging coverage drops three Sundays in a row points to a scheduling gap, not a volunteer failure. The same room consistently hitting capacity suggests you need a third adult or a lower enrollment cap. Patterns require your action—adjusting the schedule, retraining, or escalating to the Safeguard Team.

A serious or repeated individual variance requires a coaching conversation. If one volunteer keeps arriving late, leaving early, or breaking the two-adult rule, that is a performance issue. Address it directly, document the conversation, and if the behavior continues, escalate to the Safeguard Team for possible removal.

The Rule

Variance logs are transparency tools, not discipline tools. Never punish a volunteer for logging a variance. If volunteers fear logging, they will stop logging—and your visibility disappears.

Section 5

The Annual Compliance Audit

Once a year, the Safeguard Team coordinates a compliance review of every ministry area. As a leader, you walk through a checklist covering screening files, training records, ratio compliance, documentation practices, and records storage.

The audit is not a test you pass or fail. It is a diagnostic. Gaps found during the audit are expected—what matters is that they are found, documented, and resolved. Flag anything incomplete and build a plan to close it. Submit the completed checklist to the Safeguard Team.

Why This Matters

The annual audit is the single moment each year where the entire system gets a checkup. It catches the things that drift quietly—a volunteer whose VSC expired without anyone noticing, a room capacity card that was never updated after renovations, a variance log that stopped being reviewed. The audit does not create extra work. It reveals the work you were already supposed to be doing.

Section 6

Leader Track Complete

You have now completed the full Leader Track. You understand your five functions, where you sit in the governance chain, how to screen and manage volunteers, how to respond when something goes wrong, and how to keep the system honest through walkthroughs, variance review, and compliance audits.

The Safeguard Framework does not run on good intentions. It runs on leaders who enforce the standards, coach their teams, and follow through on every incident, every variance, and every audit finding. That leader is you.

This is not bureaucracy. This is stewardship.

Your quick-reference guide (SG-T-REF-001) is your daily companion—the “what form do I use when?” lookup table and records retention guidance. Keep it accessible.

Thank you for leading this system. The children, families, and volunteers in your care are safer because you took this seriously.
