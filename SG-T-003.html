Recognizing Abuse & Reporting It

Module 3 — Required for All Volunteers, Staff, and Leaders
SG-T-003 Version 3.0 March 2026 Bethany Chapel
⏱ Estimated reading time: 10 minutes
This is the most important module in the training. The rest of the system exists to prevent what this module teaches you to respond to. You will almost certainly never need to use what you learn here. But if the moment ever comes, what you say and do will matter more than any other procedure in this framework.
Section 1

Why the System Is Designed This Way

Every protection you have learned so far—screening, the Two-Adult Rule, visibility, ratios—exists for a reason. They are not bureaucracy. They are designed to interrupt a specific pattern.

Abuse in institutional settings almost never begins with abuse. It begins with access: an adult earns trust, builds a reputation, and slowly creates opportunities to be alone with a child. Over time, boundaries get pushed. By the time anything visible happens, the groundwork has been laid for weeks or months.

How the System Interrupts This

Screening ensures vetting before anyone gains access.

The Two-Adult Rule eliminates isolation.

Visibility removes secrecy—open doors, uncovered windows, no blind spots.

Communication boundaries prevent private contact between adults and minors.

Documentation creates accountability that cannot be talked away.

This is why an adult who consistently resists these procedures—who objects to the Two-Adult Rule, wants to close doors, or avoids documentation—is exhibiting the exact behaviour the system is designed to catch. That kind of resistance is worth mentioning to your ministry leader, even if the person seems well-intentioned.

Section 2

If a Child Tells You Something

Scenario

You are cleaning up after the elementary program. A nine-year-old named Lily is waiting for her mom, drawing quietly. Without looking up, she says, “My uncle plays a game with me at night that I don’t like.”

You have about five seconds to respond. What you say next matters more than almost anything you will ever do in ministry.

Most disclosures do not sound like disclosures. Children use fragments, indirect statements, or questions that test whether you are safe enough to tell. A child might say “I don’t want to go home,” or “My friend’s dad does something bad to her”—the “friend” is often the child herself. Be attentive to all of these.

What to Say

“I believe you.”

“This is not your fault.”

“I’m going to make sure you are safe.”

You do not need to say anything more. These three sentences validate the child, remove blame, and promise action.

What NOT to Say

“Are you sure?” — communicates doubt. “Why didn’t you tell someone sooner?” — adds guilt. “I’ll keep this between us” — you cannot promise secrecy; you have a legal duty to report. “What exactly happened?” — do not investigate or ask leading questions. And stay calm—visible shock can frighten a child into silence.

Section 3

The Four Reporting Steps

Under Alberta law, any adult who has reasonable grounds to suspect a child is in need of intervention must report to Children’s Services or the Police. This is not optional. You do not need permission from your leader. You do not need certainty. You need reasonable grounds.

You are never in trouble for reporting.

You are always in trouble for not reporting.

  1. Ensure Safety If the child is in immediate danger, call 911. Remove the child from the unsafe situation if you can do so safely. Do not return a child to a person you suspect of abuse.
  2. Report to Authorities Call Children’s Services or the Police immediately. Do not wait until the end of the service. Do not wait until you have talked to your ministry leader. If you are unsure whether your concern meets the threshold, call anyway—they will help you assess.
  3. Notify Your Ministry Leader After you have contacted authorities, notify the Safeguard Team or your ministry leader. Leadership notification comes after the external report, not before.
  4. Document Complete the Suspected Abuse Report (SG-FRM-007) the same day. Use the child’s own words. Record only facts—what was said, what you observed, what you did. No opinions. No diagnoses.
Why Authorities Come Before Leadership

If you notify leadership first, there is a risk the report gets delayed or filtered. The legal report goes out first. Always. Your ministry leader supports you after the report is made, not as a gatekeeper before it.

What NOT to Do

Do not investigate. Do not confront the alleged abuser. Do not notify a parent if the parent is the suspected abuser. Do not delay. Do not promise secrecy. Do not discuss the situation with other volunteers or parents. And do not carry this alone—after the report is made, talk to your ministry leader and seek support for yourself.

If you are a Junior Volunteer (under 16): Tell your ministry leader or the Safeguard Team immediately. The adult leader will ensure the formal report is made.

Section 4

Accidents & Incidents

Not every injury is abuse—kids scrape their knees, bump their heads, and get into scuffles. But every incident must be documented, no matter how minor. The rule is simple: if a parent would want to know about it, document it.

Use the Incident/Accident Report (SG-FRM-006) whenever a child is injured, a behavioural incident occurs, or a medical situation arises. Write facts only: what happened, when, what was done, who was present. Notify the parent at pickup.

SG-FRM-006
Incident / Accident Report
Scraped knee, bumped head, behavioural incident, allergic reaction—anything that happened during program that a parent needs to know about.
SG-FRM-007
Suspected Abuse Report
A child discloses abuse, you observe signs that concern you, or you witness behaviour by an adult that violates safeguarding standards.

If you are ever unsure which form to use, ask your ministry leader. When in doubt, document more rather than less.
