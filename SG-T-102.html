Screening & Managing Volunteers

Leader Track — Module 2 of 3
SG-T-102 Version 1.0 March 2026 Bethany Chapel
⏱ Estimated reading time: 10 minutes
Your volunteers learned the screening process from the applicant’s side. This module teaches you the other side—running the pipeline, conducting the interview, making the hard calls, handling early-service exceptions, and tracking renewals.
Section 1

The Gate You Guard

Scenario

A well-liked church member approaches you after the service. “I want to help in children’s ministry. My kids are in the program, I’m here every Sunday, and honestly—you look like you need the help.” He’s right. You do need the help. He seems like a great guy. Everyone likes him.

But “seems like a great guy” is not a screening process. Your job is to welcome his interest and walk him through every step—no shortcuts, no fast-tracking, no matter how urgent the staffing need.

Screening is the first and most important protection in the entire framework. Every other safeguard—ratios, the two-adult rule, reporting—assumes that the adults in the room have been vetted. If the gate fails, everything downstream is compromised.

Section 2

The Onboarding Pipeline

The Screening & Onboarding Checklist (SG-SOP-001) defines seven steps. Every step must be completed before a person serves. You are responsible for ensuring nothing is skipped, rushed, or left open.

Step 1
Application
Applicant completes the Ministry Application. Confirm at least six months of regular attendance at Bethany Chapel. No exceptions to the attendance requirement without Elder approval.
SG-FRM-001
Step 2
Interview
A 10–15 minute in-person conversation using the Interview Guide built into SG-SOP-001. This is your most important step and gets its own section below.
SG-SOP-001 (Interview Guide)
Step 3
References
Collect and contact two references using the Reference Check Form. Do not accept references from family members. Read the completed forms yourself—do not delegate this to someone outside the screening process.
SG-FRM-002
Step 4
Police Information Check
Applicant signs the consent form and submits for a Vulnerable Sector Check. Bethany covers the cost. Do not allow anyone to serve before results are received and filed—not even in a “shadowing” capacity, unless they hold an approved early-service exception.
SG-FRM-003
Step 5
Worker’s Covenant
The applicant reads and signs the Worker’s Covenant, committing to uphold the Safeguard Framework standards. This is not a formality. If someone hesitates to sign, that is information worth paying attention to.
SG-FRM-004
Step 6
Training
The applicant completes the full Volunteer Track (SG-T-001 through SG-T-004) and passes all comprehension checks at 80% or higher.
SG-T-001 – SG-T-004
Step 7
Final Approval
You confirm the file is complete and recommend the applicant. The Safeguard Team reviews and signs off. Only then is the person approved for service.
SG-SOP-001 (Approval section)
The Rule

No one serves until every step is complete and the Safeguard Team confirms approval. Staffing pressure is never a reason to skip or accelerate any step.

Section 3

Conducting the Screening Interview

The Interview Guide in SG-SOP-001 gives you the questions. This section teaches you how to use them.

The interview is a 10–15 minute conversation, not an interrogation. Your goal is to get a sense of the person—their faith, their motivation, their self-awareness, and their honesty. You are not looking for perfect answers. You are looking for authenticity and readiness.

Set the Tone

Start warm. Thank them for their interest. Explain that everyone who serves goes through this process because the children and families we serve deserve that level of care. Frame the interview as part of how we steward trust—not as a barrier to entry.

Listen for Motivation

Why do they want to serve with children? Most answers are fine: “I love kids,” “My own children are in the program,” “I want to give back.” What you are listening for are answers that centre on access rather than service—an unusual insistence on a specific age group, a desire to work alone, or an inability to articulate why they want to serve.

Listen for Self-Awareness

The character questions ask about frustration, feedback, and teamwork. Strong candidates acknowledge their weaknesses. Be cautious of someone who presents themselves as having no struggles, no frustrations, and no need for growth.

Listen for Honesty on the Integrity Questions

SG-SOP-001 asks whether there are past experiences or personal challenges that could affect their ability to serve. This question exists for a reason. A person with a relevant history who discloses it honestly may still be appropriate for service in a different capacity. A person who conceals it is a risk.

Why This Matters

The interview is where you encounter the grooming pattern at its earliest point. Abusers seek positions of trust with children. They are often charming, eager, and available. The screening process—especially the interview—is designed to slow that pattern down and make it visible. You are not being suspicious. You are being faithful to the children in your care.

Section 4

Making a “Not Yet” or “No” Decision

Not every applicant will be approved. This is one of the hardest parts of your role, especially in a church where relationships matter and rejection feels unkind.

Scenario

A long-time church member completes the application, but one of her references raises a concern about her temper with children. You notice the same pattern during the interview—she dismisses the idea that children can be frustrating. You like her. She has been at Bethany for years. But the screening process exists for moments exactly like this.

“Not yet” is appropriate when the person needs more time, more maturity, or more understanding of the role before serving. Be specific: “We’d love to revisit this in six months after you’ve had a chance to attend our parenting workshop.”

“No” is appropriate when references, the VSC, or the interview reveal a concern that would place children at risk. You do not need to justify this decision in detail. A simple, compassionate response is enough: “We appreciate your heart for serving. Based on the screening process, we’re going to direct you toward other ministry opportunities that would be a better fit.”

When a Referral Comes from a Pastor or Elder

Social pressure does not override the screening process. If an elder refers someone and the interview raises a concern, you flag it to the Safeguard Team. The process is the same for everyone. That consistency is the entire point.

Document your reasoning. A brief note in the applicant’s file—“Interview conducted [date]. Concerns noted regarding [specific area]. Recommended: not approved at this time / approved with conditions / declined”—protects you, protects the church, and creates a record if the person reapplies later.

Section 5

Early-Service Exceptions

In limited cases, the Safeguard Team may grant an early-service exception—typically for a mature believer transferring from another church of the same denomination. As a leader, you need to know the constraints:

Requires three satisfactory references, at least one from a previous pastor or ministry director.

Requires written Safeguard Team approval and clear marking on all schedules as “early-service (in process).”

Serves under direct supervision only. Does not count toward the Two-Adult Rule or ratios. Must never be alone with children or lead independently.

The exception ends when the full screening process is complete—including the six-month attendance requirement.

Do not request early-service exceptions to solve staffing problems. The exception exists for genuinely exceptional circumstances—not for convenience.

Section 6

Tracking Renewals

Screening is not a one-time event. Your responsibility does not end when a volunteer is approved—it continues every year.

Annual Renewal Requirements

Refresher training — completed each ministry year (in person or online).

Policy review and acknowledgement — SG-FRM-004 re-signed annually.

Police check renewal — every three years. The Safeguard Team tracks this and notifies you when a volunteer’s check is coming due.

Check compliance status before adding anyone to a schedule. If a volunteer’s training has lapsed or their VSC is expired, they do not serve—regardless of how much you need them. The Safeguard Team maintains the master compliance record. When in doubt, confirm with them.

A lapsed volunteer is not in trouble. They simply pause until their renewal is complete. Your job is to make sure no one who has lapsed ends up on a schedule, and to help them get current quickly when they are ready to return.

Comprehension Check

Test Your Understanding

Answer all five questions, then submit. You need 80% (4 out of 5) to pass this module.

1. During a screening interview, an applicant says he specifically wants to work with 10–12 year old boys and is not interested in any other age group. He cannot clearly explain why. What should you do?
2. A volunteer’s police check results have not come back yet, but your nursery is critically short-staffed this Sunday. The volunteer has completed all other screening steps. What do you do?
3. An Elder recommends a new attendee for children’s ministry. The attendee has been at Bethany for three months. What is the correct response?
4. You complete a screening interview and feel uneasy, but you cannot point to a single specific red flag. The applicant’s references were positive. What should you do?
5. An early-service exception volunteer has been serving for two months. Their six-month attendance mark is still three months away. Another leader asks to schedule them as one of the two required adults in a toddler room. What do you say?
