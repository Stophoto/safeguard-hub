Supervision: Two-Adult Rule, Ratios & High-Risk Settings

Module 2 — Required for All Volunteers, Staff, and Leaders
SG-T-002 Version 3.0 March 2026 Bethany Chapel
⏱ Estimated reading time: 10 minutes
This module covers three of the six core protections: the Two-Adult Rule, supervision ratios, and bathroom and diapering protocols. These are the rules you will use every single time you serve. Know them cold.
Section 1

The Two-Adult Rule

Scenario

A seven-year-old asks you to help her find her jacket in the hallway closet. Your co-leader is still inside with the group. For ninety seconds, you would be alone with a child in an empty hallway.

Ninety seconds. That is all it takes to create a situation the Safeguard Framework is designed to prevent.

Two approved adults must always be present when supervising children, youth, or vulnerable adults. In every room, on every trip, at every event, without exception.

An approved adult is someone 18 or older who has completed the full screening and training process. Youth helpers (under 18) are valued, but they do not count toward the two-adult minimum. You must always have two approved adults present, even if youth helpers are also in the room.

This rule protects children—a child is never isolated with a single adult. It also protects you—when two adults are always present, there is always a witness.

Observable and Interruptible

The Two-Adult Rule is not just about headcount. Every ministry activity must be observable (anyone walking by can see what is happening) and interruptible (anyone can step in at any time).

Keep It Visible

Doors stay open. If a room has a clear, unobstructed window, the door may be closed. Otherwise, it stays open.

Windows stay uncovered. Never cover, block, or obstruct windows in doors or walls.

No blind spots. Position yourself and your co-leader in different areas of the room so every child is in line of sight.

Designated spaces only. Do not move children to unscheduled rooms, storage areas, or private offices.

The Test

Could a parent or leader walk by right now and see exactly what is happening? If not, something needs to change immediately.

What Isolation Looks Like

Isolation is any situation where an adult and a child are together without visibility or a second approved adult. All of the following are violations: taking a child to a hallway or closet alone, meeting one-on-one in a closed office, driving alone with an unrelated minor, walking a child to the parking lot alone, or continuing to supervise after your co-leader has left the room.

If you find yourself about to be alone with a child, stop. Bring the child back to the group or call for another adult. It is always better to pause than to create an isolated situation, even for a moment.

Section 2

When Coverage Drops

Scenario

You arrive at 8:45 to set up. Your co-leader was due at 8:30. It’s now 8:50 and parents are arriving. Your co-leader hasn’t texted.

You are one adult in a room. What do you do?

Coverage will drop at some point. A co-leader is late, or needs a bathroom break, or gets called away. What matters is what you do when it happens. Follow one procedure:

1
Pause Stop receiving new children. If your co-leader just stepped out briefly and the room has a window or open door, stay put—you are still visible. If not, move the group to a visible, public space.
2
Merge Call for help. Contact a floater, merge your group with an adjacent room, or notify your ministry leader to reassign coverage.
3
Log Once resolved, record the event on the Exception/Variance Log. This is not punishment—it is a transparency tool so leadership can fix scheduling problems.
Important

Logging a variance is never discipline. You are never in trouble for documenting what happened. If coverage drops keep happening, that is a scheduling problem—not a volunteer problem.

Section 3

Supervision Ratios

The Two-Adult Rule sets the floor: always at least two approved adults. Supervision ratios go further—they define how many children each adult can safely supervise based on age. A room with two adults and thirty toddlers meets the Two-Adult Rule but is not safe. Ratios fill that gap.

You do not need to memorize the entire table. Know the ratio for the age group you serve.

Age Group Target Ratio Capacity (2 Leaders)
Infants (0–12 months)1 : 36 infants
Toddlers (13–35 months)1 : 4–58–10 toddlers
Preschool (3 yrs – K)1 : 6–812–16 children
Elementary (Gr. 1–6)1 : 7–1214–24 children
Youth (Gr. 7–12)1 : 10–1220–24 youth
Vulnerable Adults1 : 6–812–16 adults

Youth helpers (under 18) count toward ratio calculations once the Two-Adult Rule is already met. So if you have two approved adults and one youth helper in a preschool room at 1:8, your capacity is 24 children (3 supervisors × 8).

Room Capacity Cards

Every room has a Room Capacity Card (SG-SOP-003) posted near the door. It shows the maximum number of children your room can hold based on how many leaders are present. Check it at the start of every shift.

When the Room Is Full

Post the “Room Full” sign on or near the door. Stop receiving additional children and redirect parents to the check-in desk. Notify your ministry leader—they may reassign another leader or open an overflow room.

Never exceed your capacity. Accepting “just one more” when you are at your limit is a ratio violation. The capacity number is there for a reason.

Section 4

Bathrooms & Diapering

Scenario

A four-year-old says, “I have to go potty.” The in-room bathroom is occupied. The nearest hallway bathroom is around the corner. Your co-leader is reading a story to twelve other children.

What do you do? The answer depends on knowing the protocol—not making it up in the moment.

Bathrooms involve privacy, physical vulnerability, and the potential for isolation. Every bathroom interaction must follow a clear protocol. The Washroom Escort Protocol (SG-SOP-002) is posted near every bathroom as your quick reference.

Core Principles — All Ages

Visibility at all times. Doors open, windows clear.

No isolation. An adult must never be alone with a child behind a closed bathroom door.

Never enter a stall with a child.

Wash hands before and after any diapering or toileting support.

When in doubt, call the parent.

Nursery: Diapering (Ages 0–2)

Only designated, screened female adults may change diapers. Male volunteers are not permitted unless caring for their own child. Youth helpers are not permitted to change diapers or assist with toileting.

Diapering must occur at approved changing stations only. The nursery door must remain open. A second approved adult must be present or within direct line of sight. Parents may opt out of diapering and request to be contacted instead.

Preschool: Bathroom Use (Ages 2–5)

In-room bathrooms (standard): Prop the door slightly open so the child is audible. Stand near the doorway, not inside. Provide verbal guidance first; only enter if absolutely necessary, with the door open.

Hallway bathrooms (contingency): Use a single-stall family washroom if available—the child enters, you wait outside. In a multi-stall restroom, wait in the common sink area, visible to anyone entering. Never enter a closed stall with a child. If physical assistance is needed, two approved adults must be present in the bathroom area.

Elementary: Independent Use (Grades 1–6)

Elementary children use the bathroom independently. The child tells a leader before leaving. Track the time. If a child does not return within five to seven minutes, call from outside the bathroom door—do not enter.
